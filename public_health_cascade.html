The Public Health Cascade
Day 1, 11:45 AM · State Department of Public Health · Executive Simulation
Your Role
Incident Summary
Agency
State Department of Public Health. You oversee communicable disease response for a state of 4.2 million people.
AI System
ContaTrace-AI. Deployed 14 months ago for contact tracing prioritization and outbreak resource allocation. Analyzes case data, compliance history, and demographic indicators to direct field team resources.
The Flaw
ContaTrace-AI deprioritized follow-up in three ZIP codes based on "historical compliance rates." Those rates were low — but the cause was language barriers and documented fear of immigration enforcement, not genuine non-compliance. The system learned the outcome, not the cause.
What Happened
ZIP Codes 07801, 07802, 07803
Majority Immigrant Communities
Three adjacent ZIP codes with large Central American immigrant populations. Prior outbreak response data showed low compliance — driven by language barriers and fear, not refusal.
ContaTrace-AI Output
Resource Allocation Decision
System deprioritized these ZIP codes for follow-up, directing field teams elsewhere. Your Deputy Commissioner used this output to make documented resource allocation decisions.
The Gap
11 Days Without Escalation
A respiratory illness cluster in ZIP 07801 was not flagged for 11 days. During that window: 14 hospitalizations, 2 deaths, 87 additional cases identified retroactively.
Salud Para Todos
Community Health Organization
A community health organization has gone public with the story. A state legislator is calling for an investigation. Local media has the story for tonight's broadcast.
What You Know and Must Reckon With
ContaTrace-AI encoded historical inequity and acted on it. The system did not know why compliance rates were low. It only knew that they were. It treated a structural barrier as a behavioral pattern — and your organization deployed it without asking whether the training data reflected reality or reflected the consequences of prior underservice. Two people died during the gap. Fourteen families have a family member who was hospitalized. The communities most harmed are the ones who have the least historical reason to trust the public health system.
You Have Three Decisions to Make
A community forum has been requested. A legislative hearing is coming. Your deputy commissioner is in your office. Each decision will define how your agency, and the field, responds to algorithmic harm in public health — and whether the communities affected have any reason to trust you going forward.
Read carefully. You cannot return to the briefing once you begin.
Decision 1 of 3
Day 1 — 11:45 AM
The Deputy Commissioner
Your Deputy Commissioner is in your office. She is the person whose resource allocation decisions, based on ContaTrace-AI output, are now in the legislative record. She did not question the system's output — it was presented to her as the optimized allocation recommendation. She followed it. She is not certain whether that was the right decision given the data she had. You have a press briefing at 1pm and a community organization calling for a public forum. You need to decide your posture toward her — and toward the system — before noon.
As State Health Officer: Your deputy followed the system. You deployed the system without ensuring field teams could question it. The accountability here is layered. How you treat her — publicly and privately — defines your organization's relationship with internal decision-making for years.
What do you do with your Deputy Commissioner — and how do you characterize the system's role publicly?
